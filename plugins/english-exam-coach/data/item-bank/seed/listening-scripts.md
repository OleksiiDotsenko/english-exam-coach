# Seed listening scripts (original examples)

All scripts are original, written for this plugin as format references. They
are NOT from any official exam. In a live drill: give the learner the
questions first, then deliver the script (TTS if available, otherwise the
learner reads it once at natural speed or has someone read it aloud), then
collect answers before revealing the key.

## Everyday dialogue + form completion (IELTS Part 1 style, B1–B2)

**Questions (complete the booking form, one word/number per gap):**
1. Class type: ____ for beginners
2. Day: ____ evenings
3. Start time: ____
4. Price per term: £ ____

**Script:**
> **Receptionist:** Riverside Community Centre, good afternoon.
> **Caller:** Hi — I'm ringing about evening classes. Do you still run the
> pottery class for beginners?
> **Receptionist:** We do. That one meets on Thursday evenings — it used to
> be Tuesdays, but we moved it this term.
> **Caller:** Thursday works. What time does it start?
> **Receptionist:** Half past six, and it runs for ninety minutes.
> **Caller:** And the cost?
> **Receptionist:** It's ninety-five pounds for the term, materials included.

**Key:** 1 pottery · 2 Thursday · 3 6:30 (half past six) · 4 95

## Mini-lecture + multiple choice (TOEFL / IELTS Part 4 style, B2–C1)

**Questions:**
1. According to the speaker, early canal builders lined tunnels with clay
   mainly to…
   A. strengthen the walls · B. keep water from escaping ✅ · C. smooth the
   surface for boats
2. The speaker mentions candle flames as a way of… *(detecting air flow ✅ /
   lighting the workface / signalling between crews)*
3. What surprised modern engineers who surveyed the tunnel? *(how accurate
   the original alignment was ✅)*

**Script:**
> When the Marlton canal tunnel was cut in the 1790s, engineers faced two
> quiet enemies: water and air. Water first — the tunnel passed through
> porous sandstone, so the diggers lined every metre with puddled clay, not
> to hold the roof up, as people often assume, but to stop the canal simply
> draining away into the rock. Air was the subtler problem. With shafts only
> every half mile, crews judged ventilation by candle flame: a flame leaning
> steadily meant air was moving; a still flame meant danger. What impresses
> surveyors today, though, is none of this — it's that two teams digging
> from opposite ends met in the middle barely a hand's width out of line.

**Key:** 1 B · 2 detecting air flow · 3 the accuracy of the alignment
