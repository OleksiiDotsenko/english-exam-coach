# Speaking calibration anchors — leveled transcript excerpts (original)

Four original spoken-answer transcripts to ONE shared question, each typical
of its CEFR level, with hesitation phenomena written out (fillers, restarts,
self-corrections) because that is exactly where speaking level shows. Use
them to anchor speaking-band judgments so estimates stay consistent across
sessions: place the learner's transcript next to the nearest anchor(s) per
aspect (range, accuracy, fluency, interaction, coherence) BEFORE fixing a
range. These are demonstration transcripts written for this plugin — not
official samples, not model answers to memorise.

**Shared prompt:** *"Describe a skill you would like to learn, and say why."*

## B1

> Er… I would like to learn, um, how to cook. Because now I eat, uh, a lot of
> fast food and it's not good, I think. My mother, she cooks very well, so
> maybe she can teach me. I want to make, er… traditional food from my
> country. It's — it's not easy but I think if I practise every week I can
> do it.

**Why B1:** the message is clear and connected with simple linkers (*because,
so, but*), but fillers are frequent, sentences are short and mostly present
tense, vocabulary is basic (*good, not easy, very well*), and there is little
elaboration.

## B2

> I'd really like to learn to edit videos properly. At the moment I just, um,
> record clips on my phone and post them, but they look quite amateur — the
> pacing is off, the sound is bad. If I could edit well, I could actually
> tell a story instead of just, you know, showing something. I've watched a
> few tutorials, but what I really need is to sit down and practise on a real
> project.

**Why B2:** fluent stretches with only natural hesitation, a range of
structures including a conditional and a contrast, topic-appropriate lexis
(*pacing, amateur, tell a story*), and clear organisation — but the ideas
stay fairly concrete and the self-repair is still visible.

## C1

> The skill I keep coming back to is public speaking — not presenting slides,
> but genuinely holding a room. I can write a decent argument, yet the moment
> I'm on my feet I rush, and whatever nuance I had on paper just… evaporates.
> What draws me to it is that it's almost purely a matter of practice and
> nerve rather than talent, which means it's learnable; I'd just have to be
> willing to be bad at it in front of people for a while.

**Why C1:** flexible, spontaneous delivery with controlled self-correction
used for effect, precise and slightly abstract lexis (*nuance, evaporates,
holding a room*), and a developed, well-organised point — hesitation serves
the meaning rather than betraying difficulty.

## C2

> If I could pick one, it would be sight-reading music — sitting at a piano
> and simply playing something I've never seen. What fascinates me is that
> it collapses a gap most of us live with: the lag between understanding a
> thing and being able to do it. A fluent sight-reader isn't decoding and
> then executing; the two have fused. I suspect learning it would quietly
> change how I think about every other skill I've ever laboured over.

**Why C2:** effortless flow, idiomatic and metaphorical precision deployed
with purpose (*collapses a gap, quietly change*), a shaped argument delivered
without visible strain — the language serves the idea completely.

## How to use the anchors

1. Match the learner's transcript to the nearest anchor **per aspect** — a
   speaker can be C1 for range but B2 for fluency; say so explicitly.
2. These monologue anchors do **not** calibrate the **interaction** aspect:
   every anchor answers a single-speaker prompt, so none shows turn-taking,
   holding the floor, or responding to a partner. For interactive tasks
   (IELTS Part 1/3, Cambridge collaborative discussion) judge interaction
   directly from the interaction descriptors in `speaking-descriptors.md`,
   and against a paired sample when one is available.
3. Judge fluency only from a transcript that preserves fillers and
   self-corrections; if it does not, say fluency is assessed provisionally.
4. Derive the CEFR estimate from anchor proximity, then convert to the
   exam's scale; report a range, not a point.
5. Never present the anchor transcripts to the learner as model answers to
   imitate — they calibrate the coach, not the learner.
