# Reading calibration anchors — leveled passage samples (original)

One shared topic written as a short passage at each CEFR level, so a
generated reading text sits at the level it claims. `reading-descriptors.md`
says what a *reader* can do; these show what the *text* looks like. Before
generating a passage, match your target to the nearest anchor on the four
levers below; after generating, check it against them. These are original
demonstration texts, not official samples.

**The four levers (what actually moves a text up a level):**

1. **Lexical frequency** — how far beyond the ~2,000 most common words it
   reaches (topic words, low-frequency verbs, abstract nouns).
2. **Sentence/clause complexity** — clause depth, subordination, embedding,
   fronting, non-finite clauses.
3. **Idiom / figurative load** — metaphor, collocation, implied meaning.
4. **Idea density & abstraction** — concrete facts vs argument, nuance,
   information you must infer rather than read off the surface.

**Shared topic:** *urban rooftop gardens.*

## B1

> More and more people in cities are growing plants on their roofs. A rooftop
> garden is a good way to use space that is usually empty. People grow
> vegetables, herbs and flowers there. The plants also keep the building
> cooler in summer, so people use less electricity. Some neighbours share one
> garden and look after it together. It is hard work, but many people say it
> makes them feel calmer and helps them meet others.

**Why B1:** high-frequency vocabulary, short mostly single-clause sentences
with simple linkers (*so, but*), no idiom, concrete facts stated directly.

## B2

> Rooftop gardens are becoming a familiar sight in dense cities where ground
> space is scarce. Besides providing fresh produce, they insulate the
> buildings beneath them, cutting cooling costs noticeably in summer.
> Advocates point out that shared gardens can strengthen a sense of community,
> giving neighbours a reason to cooperate. The main obstacles are practical:
> roofs must be able to bear the extra weight, and watering can be awkward
> several storeys up.

**Why B2:** broader topic vocabulary (*dense, scarce, insulate, advocates*),
some complex sentences with subordination, mild abstraction (community,
obstacles), still largely explicit.

## C1

> As cities densify, the flat roofs that once went unused are increasingly
> being reimagined as productive green space. The appeal is more than
> aesthetic: a well-planted roof buffers the building against summer heat,
> trims energy bills, and slows the rainwater that would otherwise overwhelm
> drains. What is harder to quantify — and what enthusiasts tend to stress —
> is the social dividend, the way a shared plot can knit together residents
> who might otherwise never speak.

**Why C1:** lower-frequency and abstract lexis (*densify, buffers, dividend*),
flexible structures (fronting, dashes, non-finite clauses), an argument with
a concede-and-qualify move, meaning that must be partly inferred.

## C2

> That a city's rooftops should have sat idle for so long says less about
> engineering than about habit. Reclaiming them turns out to pay in several
> currencies at once: cooler buildings, lighter energy loads, storm water
> held back from straining drains — and, less tangibly, a loosening of the
> anonymity that dense living breeds. None of this is free; roofs must carry
> the load and water must somehow climb. But the calculus increasingly
> favours the garden, and the empty roof is starting to look like the
> anomaly.

**Why C2:** dense, idiomatic and figurative (*pay in several currencies, the
anonymity that dense living breeds, the calculus favours*), sustained
abstraction, information compressed so the reader must unpack it, a shaped
argument rather than a list of facts.

## How to use

1. Pick the level; write the passage to match its lever profile, not just its
   topic. A "C2 passage" that reads like the B2 anchor is mis-leveled even if
   the questions are hard.
2. For gapped text / multiple matching, apply the same levers to the removed
   paragraphs and the surrounding text.
3. Never show these anchors to the learner as the reading passage — they
   calibrate the generator, not the drill.
